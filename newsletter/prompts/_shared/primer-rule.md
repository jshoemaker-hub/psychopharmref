# Primer rule for unfamiliar named entities

The first time you introduce a specific named entity that a **generalist practicing psychiatrist — anywhere in the world** — might not immediately recognize, follow the first mention with a one- to two-sentence parenthetical primer. The primer states (a) what the entity is and (b) why a reader should care in one breath, then stops. Do not lecture. Do not repeat the primer on later mentions of the same entity in the same letter.

## What triggers a primer

- Pipeline drug code names (e.g., "NBI-1117568") or new brand names not yet in broad clinical use.
- Specific bills or public laws by number or short title ("the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act," "H.R. 2364").
- Specific FDA, EMA, MHRA, TGA, Health Canada, or PMDA actions referred to by their internal names — REMS changes, labeling changes, advisory committee names, NICE TA numbers, PBAC listings.
- US-specific payors, regulators, or policy bodies a non-US reader might not know — CMS, Medicaid, the VA, state medical boards, scope-of-practice laws, SAMHSA, ACA.
- Non-US-specific bodies a US reader might not know — NICE, MHRA, TGA, PBAC, Health Canada's NOC, EMA CHMP.
- Rating-scale eponyms used without the acronym spelled out (Hamilton, MADRS, PANSS, YBOCS, C-SSRS, Y-MRS, AIMS).
- Historical figures, labs, or institutions central to the piece (Laborit, Cade, Kuhn, Cerletti, Sainte-Anne, Münsterlingen, the Decade of the Brain).
- Landmark trials referenced by acronym (STAR*D, CATIE, CUtLASS, EUFEST, RAISE, TADS, TMAP, CATIE-AD, TURNS).

## What does NOT need a primer

Anything a generalist psychiatrist practicing anywhere would already know:
- Drug classes in routine use (SSRI, SNRI, TCA, MAOI, typical/atypical antipsychotic, benzodiazepine, mood stabilizer, stimulant).
- The household-name drugs (Prozac/fluoxetine, lithium, haloperidol, clozapine, methylphenidate, diazepam).
- The field's top-level institutions by acronym (FDA, EMA, MHRA, WHO, APA, AACAP, ICD, DSM).
- The common diagnostic entities (MDD, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, OCD, PTSD, ADHD, generalized anxiety disorder).

## Form — strict

A primer is an **inline parenthetical**, maximum **one or two sentences**, placed immediately after the first mention of the named entity. It is never a standalone sentence, never a standalone paragraph, and never a preamble that delays the clinical content.

Correct:
  "…the trial used the HAM-D (the Hamilton Depression Rating Scale, a 17-item clinician-administered scale from 1960 that remains the most common comparator in antidepressant trials) as its primary outcome…"

Correct:
  "…esketamine carries a REMS (a mandatory FDA safety program that requires in-clinic administration and observation for this drug class)…"

Forbidden:
- A whole paragraph explaining what the entity is before the clinical content begins.
- A note like "A brief note on terminology for international readers: …" — never use that framing. The primer is inline; the reader does not need to be told a primer is coming.
- A primer that restates what was just said ("the FDA (the Food and Drug Administration)") — if the term is in the non-primer list below, it gets no primer at all.
- Two primers in the same sentence — if both terms need explaining, rework the sentence so only the more important one is primered on first mention.

Do not start a primer with "this is" or "which is." Begin with a noun phrase.

## Cap

At most three primers per section. If the brief surfaces more than three primer-worthy entities, pick the three most load-bearing ones and use bare mentions for the rest.

## Judgment call

If you are unsure whether something warrants a primer, err on the side of including one — but keep it inline and brief. A section that reads like a glossary is a section that has forgotten the clinical story it was supposed to tell.
