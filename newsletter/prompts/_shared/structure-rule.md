# Structure rule: substance first, deferrals last

A newsletter section delivers clinical value in proportion to how quickly it gets to the actual story. Two hard rules follow.

## Opening paragraph must deliver substance, not deferrals

The first paragraph of every section must contain specific clinical content — a named drug, a specific trial, a concrete adverse-event scenario, a named piece of legislation, a specific rating scale, a historical figure and year. Do not open with sentences that merely point the reader elsewhere.

Forbidden patterns for the opening paragraph:
- "Clinicians seeking real-time specifics should consult [database X]…"
- "Readers interested in this topic are encouraged to review [source Y]…"
- "For comprehensive information on this subject, see [resource Z]…"
- "This section will be updated in a future issue…" (apology framing)
- Any variation of "I don't have enough data to cover this properly" framing

If the research brief does not contain enough substance to open with a specific clinical fact, the prompt-specific fallback framing takes over (retrospective, historical, receptor-level, scale-history, evergreen). In no case should the piece lead with a deferral.

## External-resource referrals go at the very end

Specific databases, registries, guideline bodies, handbook titles, and "further reading" pointers appear in the final paragraph of the section only — not in the opening, not scattered through the middle. The section earns the referral by first delivering the substantive content.

Allowed at the end:
- "Clinicians wanting current figures can consult [FDA Drug Shortages database / ASHP / Orange Book / ClinicalTrials.gov / Cochrane Handbook / …]."
- "The full text of [bill / label change / guideline] is at [URL]."
- The existing PsychoPharmRef "Further reading" sentence (this is injected by the pipeline).

Inline citations to the brief's sources — formatted as (Source Name, YYYY) — are NOT deferrals and may appear anywhere. A citation anchors a claim the piece is already making; a deferral asks the reader to go elsewhere for the claim itself.

## Heuristic: if you catch yourself writing "consult" or "refer" in the first half of the section, you are deferring. Rewrite.
