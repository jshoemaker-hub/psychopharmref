            ## 1. Historical Evolution: From Melancholia to Modern Neuroscience

            <p>Depression has been recognized for millennia. Hippocrates (460–370 BCE) described "melancholia" as an imbalance of black bile, differentiating it from mania. This conceptualization dominated Western medicine for nearly 2,000 years, reflected in Galen's four-humors theory.</p>

            ## 2. Depression Across Populations: Disparities, Presentations, and Diagnostic Challenges

            ### Pediatric Depression (Ages 6–17)
            <ul>
                <li>**Prevalence:** ~2–8% in children; ~10–15% in adolescents</li>
                <li>**Presentation differences:** Irritability more prominent than sadness; behavioral/school dysfunction often the presenting complaint</li>
                <li>**Diagnostic challenge:** Overlap with ADHD, anxiety disorders, and developmental norms; parents may minimize mood symptoms</li>
                <li>**Treatment disparities:** Underdiagnosis and undertreatment in lower-income communities; SSRIs carry FDA black-box warning for suicidality (though relative risk modest)</li>
                <li>**Cultural factors:** Some ethnic groups present depression somatically (headaches, abdominal pain); language/explanatory models vary</li>
            </ul>

            ### Adult Depression (Ages 18–64)
            <ul>
                <li>**Prevalence:** ~5–7% annual prevalence; ~12–20% lifetime</li>
                <li>**Work and family dysfunction:** Major source of presenteeism; often drives treatment-seeking</li>
                <li>**Racial/ethnic disparities:** Black Americans show higher rates of MDD but lower treatment rates; Latino populations experience barriers including language, stigma, and immigration-related stress; Asian Americans often present with somatic symptoms</li>
                <li>**Healthcare access:** Uninsured or underinsured individuals receive later diagnoses and less specialized treatment</li>
            </ul>

            ### Geriatric Depression (Age 65+)
            <ul>
                <li>**Prevalence:** ~10–15% in community-dwelling; up to 50% in long-term care facilities</li>
                <li>**Presentation:** Often masked as "vascular depression," cognitive complaints ("pseudodementia"), somatic preoccupation, anhedonia without sadness</li>
                <li>**Medical comorbidity:** Frequently coexists with heart disease, stroke, diabetes, cancer; bidirectional causality suspected</li>
                <li>**Pharmacokinetic challenges:** Polypharmacy, altered metabolism, increased sensitivity to anticholinergics and hyponatremia (SIADH)</li>
                <li>**Disparities:** Underdiagnosis common; attribution of mood changes to "normal aging"; racial minorities face additional barriers</li>
            </ul>

            ## 3. Pathophysiology: Mechanisms and Models

            ### The Monoamine Model
            <p>The classical monoamine hypothesis posits that depression results from deficiency of serotonin (5-HT), norepinephrine (NE), or dopamine (DA) at synaptic clefts. Evidence includes:</p>
            <ul>
                <li>All FDA-approved antidepressants increase one or more monoamine neurotransmitters</li>
                <li>Reserpine (depletes monoamines) induces depression</li>
                <li>Monoamine agonists (e.g., stimulants) show some antidepressant effect</li>
            </ul>
            <p>**Limitations:** Monoamine reuptake blockade occurs within hours, yet clinical improvement takes weeks; ~30–40% of patients are treatment-resistant despite adequate monoamine elevation.</p>

            ### Neuroinflammation and Cytokine Models
            <p>Emerging evidence demonstrates elevated pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) in ~30% of depressed patients. Proposed mechanisms:</p>
            <ul>
                <li>**Microglia activation:** Stress and trauma activate brain resident immune cells, releasing inflammatory mediators</li>
                <li>**Blood-brain barrier dysfunction:** Allows peripheral immune activation to reach brain</li>
                <li>**Reduced trophic support:** Inflammation suppresses BDNF and neurogenesis</li>
            </ul>
            <p>This subtype may explain poor SSRI response and the rationale for anti-inflammatory augmentation (NSAIDs, curcumin, omega-3s) or IL-6 antagonists in trials.</p>

            ### HPA-Axis Dysfunction
            <p>Depression involves dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, resulting in:</p>
            <ul>
                <li>**Elevated cortisol:** Especially in melancholic depression; dexamethasone suppression test (DST) may show non-suppression</li>
                <li>**CRH hypersecretion:** Central driver of HPA hyperactivity</li>
                <li>**Glucocorticoid receptor dysfunction:** Impaired negative feedback</li>
                <li>**Downstream effects:** Hippocampal atrophy, impaired neurogenesis, metabolic derangement</li>
            </ul>

            ### Neuroplasticity and Neurotrophic Factors
            <p>Depression is characterized by reduced brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), especially in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. This leads to:</p>
            <ul>
                <li>Impaired neurogenesis in the dentate gyrus</li>
                <li>Dendritic atrophy and reduced synaptic density</li>
                <li>Diminished cognitive function (memory, executive function)</li>
                <li>All antidepressants, psychotherapy, and exercise increase BDNF and promote neuroplasticity</li>
            </ul>

            ### Gut-Brain Axis and Microbiota
            <p>The gut microbiota influences mood through:</p>
            <ul>
                <li>**SCFA production:** Microbial fermentation produces short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate) that cross the BBB and enhance GABA signaling</li>
                <li>**Tryptophan metabolism:** Dysbiosis reduces tryptophan conversion to kynurenine and serotonin precursors</li>
                <li>**Lipopolysaccharide (LPS):** Dysbiosis permits gram-negative bacterial endotoxin translocation, triggering systemic inflammation</li>
                <li>**Vagal afferents:** Microbiota-derived signals reach the brain via the vagus nerve</li>
            </ul>

            ### Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)
            <p>**Diagnostic criteria:** Depressed mood for most days for ≥2 years (≥1 year in children/adolescents); ≥2 additional symptoms (poor appetite, hypersomnia/insomnia, low energy, low self-worth, poor concentration, hopelessness); never without symptoms for >2 months; functional impairment but usually mild to moderate.</p>
            <p>**Key distinctions from MDD:** Dysthymia is chronic but less severe; two-thirds of dysthymia patients experience superimposed MDD episodes ("double depression"). Treatment-resistance is common in dysthymia alone; combination pharmacotherapy or augmentation more frequently needed.</p>

                </div>

            ## 5. Critical Differential Diagnoses Not to Miss

            <p>Misattributing secondary causes of depression to primary MDD delays treatment and may miss treatable underlying pathology.</p>

            ### Medical Etiologies

            <div class="ba-compare-row">
                <div class="ba-compare-card">
                    <h5>Hypothyroidism</h5>
                    <p>**Presentation:** Depression + fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, bradycardia, myxedema.</p>
                    <p>**Mechanism:** Thyroid hormone essential for monoamine synthesis and receptor expression.</p>
                    <p>**Red flags:** Female, age >40, family history. Obtain TSH and free T4.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-compare-card">
                    <h5>Vitamin B12 Deficiency</h5>
                    <p>**Presentation:** Depression, cognitive impairment, paresthesias, macrocytic anemia, ataxia.</p>
                    <p>**Mechanism:** B12 required for myelin formation and monoamine synthesis.</p>
                    <p>**Red flags:** Pernicious anemia, veganism, GI surgery. Check serum B12, methylmalonic acid, homocysteine.</p>
                </div>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-compare-row">
                <div class="ba-compare-card">
                    <h5>Folate Deficiency</h5>
                    <p>**Presentation:** Depression, cognitive dysfunction, macrocytic anemia.</p>
                    <p>**Mechanism:** Folate cofactor in methylation reactions; low serum folate correlates with poor antidepressant response.</p>
                    <p>**Red flags:** Poor diet, alcohol use, malabsorption. Low serum folate, elevated homocysteine predicts poor SSRI response.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-compare-card">
                    <h5>Obstructive Sleep Apnea</h5>
                    <p>**Presentation:** Depression, daytime somnolence, witnessed apneas, hypertension.</p>
                    <p>**Mechanism:** Sleep fragmentation → HPA hyperactivity, reduced BDNF, monoamine dysregulation.</p>
                    <p>**Red flags:** Loud snoring, BMI >30, witnessed apnea. Screen with STOP-BANG; refer for sleep study.</p>
                </div>
            </div>

            ### Substance-Induced and Medication-Induced Depression

            <ul>
                <li>**Alcohol:** Chronic use depresses CNS; withdrawal hyperexcitability. "Dry drunk" syndrome may mimic depression.</li>
                <li>**Stimulant use/withdrawal:** Cocaine, methamphetamine cause depression upon cessation or chronic use.</li>
                <li>**Beta-blockers:** Propranolol, atenolol associated with depression (especially at higher doses); mechanism unclear.</li>
                <li>**Corticosteroids:** Prednisone, dexamethasone (especially >20 mg/day) induce mood disturbance; mood improves with taper.</li>
                <li>**Interferon-α:** Directly induces depression in ~30% of patients; IFN-γ pathway implicated.</li>
                <li>**Isotretinoin:** Acne medication linked to depression and suicidality; mechanism unknown.</li>
            </ul>

            <div class="ba-insight-box">
                <span class="ba-insight-icon">🔍</span>
                **Screening Strategy:** Before initiating antidepressants, obtain: TSH/free T4, CBC (B12, folate), electrolytes (especially sodium), glucose, and collateral history of sleep, substance use, and recent medication/steroid changes. Consider screening for sleep apnea (STOP-BANG) and asking about medication side effects.
            </div>

            ### Bipolar Depression and Diagnostic Dilemma
            <p>Patients with unrecognized bipolar disorder receiving antidepressant monotherapy risk mood destabilization, rapid cycling, and treatment resistance.</p>
            <ul>
                <li>**Screening:** Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ), lifetime history of elevated/expansive mood, decreased need for sleep, racing thoughts, excessive spending, or risky behavior</li>
                <li>**Key history:** Ask about family history of mania, hypomanic responses to antidepressants, or antidepressant-induced rapid cycling</li>
                <li>**Treatment difference:** Bipolar depression requires mood stabilizer ± antidepressant, not antidepressant monotherapy</li>
            </ul>

            ### Early Dementia and Pseudodementia
            <p>**Pseudodementia:** Depression mimicking cognitive decline. Depressed patients show poor effort on cognitive testing, subjective memory complaints exceeding objective deficits, rapid onset, and mood precedes cognitive symptoms.</p>
            <p>**True dementia:** Cognitive decline precedes mood symptoms; objective deficits on testing; insidious onset; antidepressants don't normalize cognition (though may improve mood).</p>
            <p>**Red flag:** Geriatric depression with new cognitive complaints warrants careful cognitive assessment to exclude early Alzheimer's, vascular dementia, or Lewy body disease.</p>

            ### Novel Agents

            <div class="ba-compare-row">
                <div class="ba-compare-card">
                    <h5>Bupropion (Dopamine/NE Reuptake Inhibitor)</h5>
                    <p>**Unique features:** Activating; no sexual dysfunction; weight loss; may elevate seizure risk (dose-dependent; contraindicated if seizure disorder).</p>
                    <p>**Use:** Apathetic/withdrawn depression; augmentation with SSRIs; smoking cessation; comorbid ADHD. Dose: 300–450 mg/day.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-compare-card">
                    <h5>Mirtazapine (Noradrenergic + 5-HT Antagonist)</h5>
                    <p>**Unique features:** Sedating; increases appetite; dual mechanism at alpha-2 and 5-HT receptors.</p>
                    <p>**Use:** Depression with insomnia, anorexia; anxiety comorbidity. Dose: 15–45 mg/day (often given qhs).</p>
                </div>
            </div>

            ### Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)
            <p>**Mechanism:** Irreversibly inhibit monoamine oxidase enzymes A and B, preventing neurotransmitter degradation. Phenelzine and tranylcypromine are irreversible; moclobemide is reversible.</p>
            <p>**Efficacy:** Superior to SSRIs in atypical depression; particularly effective for depression with rejection sensitivity and hypersomnia; may also help in panic, OCD, PTSD.</p>
            <p>**Adverse effects:** Hypertensive crisis (tyramine-containing foods: aged cheese, cured meats, fermented sauces), orthostasis, weight gain, insomnia, sexual dysfunction. Require strict dietary adherence.</p>
            <p>**Use:** Second/third-line due to complexity and MAOI-SSRI serotonin syndrome risk, but valuable in treatment-resistant atypical depression. Phenelzine 45–90 mg/day; tranylcypromine 20–60 mg/day.</p>

            <div class="ba-insight-box">
                <span class="ba-insight-icon">⚡</span>
                **MAOI-SSRI Washout:** When switching from SSRI to MAOI, observe 2-week washout (5 weeks for fluoxetine due to long half-life). Risk of serotonin syndrome exists; newer approaches use low-dose MAOI + low-dose SSRI cautiously under close monitoring.
            </div>

                    <div class="ba-bar-pct">35%</div>
                </div>
            </div>

            <p style="margin-top: 20px; font-size: 0.9em; color: var(--text-muted);">Approximate response rates for augmentation in TRD (adapted from meta-analyses).</p>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 20px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Lithium</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~50–60% response/remission rate; strongest evidence; takes 1–2 weeks to see benefit</li>
                <li>**Mechanism:** Enhances 5-HT function; promotes BDNF and neurogenesis; potentiates monoamine effects</li>
                <li>**Dosing:** 600–1,200 mg/day; therapeutic level 0.6–1.2 mEq/L</li>
                <li>**Monitoring:** Baseline renal function, TSH; repeat annually. Assess sodium intake, dehydration, drug interactions (NSAIDs, ACE-inhibitors, thiazides elevate lithium)</li>
                <li>**Toxicity:** Narrow therapeutic index; tremor, polyuria, hypothyroidism, renal impairment with chronic use</li>
            </ul>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Atypical Antipsychotics</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~50–55% response; aripiprazole, quetiapine, olanzapine FDA-approved for augmentation</li>
                <li>**Mechanism:** D2 dopamine blockade + 5-HT2A antagonism; enhance monoamine signaling</li>
                <li>**Dosing:** Lower than psychosis doses (aripiprazole 2–5 mg/day, quetiapine 25–100 mg/day at bedtime)</li>
                <li>**Drawback:** Weight gain, metabolic effects (even at low doses), akathisia, tardive dyskinesia risk (though lower at antidepressant augmentation doses)</li>
            </ul>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Thyroid Hormone (T3/Triiodothyronine)</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~50% response when added to TCAs; less robust with SSRIs; mechanism unclear</li>
                <li>**Dosing:** 25–50 mcg/day; total dose 25–100 mcg/day</li>
                <li>**Mechanism:** May enhance monoamine synthesis, BDNF, and neuroplasticity; synergizes with antidepressants</li>
                <li>**Note:** Beneficial even in euthyroid patients; doesn't require TSH elevation to work</li>
            </ul>

            ### Rapid-Acting Interventions for Severe and Treatment-Resistant Depression

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Ketamine and Esketamine (Rapid-Acting Glutamatergic Agents)</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Mechanism:** NMDA receptor antagonism; rapid AMPA receptor potentiation; increases BDNF and promotes synaptogenesis within hours</li>
                <li>**Efficacy:** 50–70% response in TRD; onset 24 hours to 1 week (much faster than conventional antidepressants)</li>
                <li>**Formulations:** IV ketamine (0.5–1 mg/kg × 40 min; weekly infusions); intranasal esketamine (Spravato: 56–84 mg twice weekly initially)</li>
                <li>**Clinical use:** Especially valuable in acute suicidal crisis; rapid mood improvement; used in inpatient and office settings</li>
                <li>**Limitations:** Dissociation during infusion; abuse potential (ketamine); expensive; esketamine requires supervised administration; long-term efficacy data limited</li>
            </ul>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Mechanism:** Repetitive TMS (rTMS) induces localized brain stimulation (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex); enhances monoamine function and promotes plasticity</li>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~40–50% response in TRD; remission ~20–30%; comparable to augmentation; noninvasive</li>
                <li>**Schedule:** Typically 5 sessions/week × 4–6 weeks; maintenance protocols available</li>
                <li>**Advantages:** No systemic side effects; cognitive benefit (vs. ECT); no seizure risk; portable devices emerging for home use</li>
                <li>**Limitations:** Modest efficacy; time commitment; scalp discomfort; cost</li>
            </ul>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Mechanism:** Generalized seizure induction under anesthesia; promotes neuroplasticity and potentiates monoamine signaling; most powerful antidepressant known</li>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~60–80% response; ~50% remission, especially in psychotic, melancholic, and catatonic depression; faster than medication (days to weeks)</li>
                <li>**Indications:** Psychotic depression, catatonia, melancholic depression with profound symptoms, high suicidality, need for rapid response, medication intolerance, prior ECT response</li>
                <li>**Administration:** Anesthetic, muscle relaxant, and ECT device; bilateral or unilateral electrode placement (unilateral has fewer cognitive effects but less efficacy)</li>
                <li>**Adverse effects:** Post-ictal confusion, transient cognitive impairment (usually fully reversible); headache, muscle ache</li>
                <li>**Maintenance:** Maintenance ECT (once weekly to monthly) improves sustained remission; often continued antidepressant therapy</li>
            </ul>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS)</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Mechanism:** Surgical implantation of electrode on vagus nerve; electrical stimulation → increased vagal signaling to brain → monoamine and BDNF enhancement</li>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~25–35% response in TRD; slow onset (6–12 months); FDA-approved for TRD</li>
                <li>**Advantages:** Durable; reversible; relatively safe; improves over time</li>
                <li>**Limitations:** Invasive surgery required; modest efficacy; cost; slow onset</li>
            </ul>

            <h4 style="color: var(--accent); margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px; font-weight: 600;">Psilocybin and Classical Psychedelics</h4>
            <ul>
                <li>**Status:** Phase 2b/3 clinical trials ongoing; preliminary data promising; FDA breakthrough therapy designation for psilocybin-assisted therapy (COMP360 program)</li>
                <li>**Mechanism:** Serotonin 2A receptor agonism; increased neuroplasticity; possible "resetting" of rigid depressive thought patterns via dissociation and psychological insight</li>
                <li>**Efficacy:** ~50–70% response in early trials; sustained benefit at 6 months post-treatment in some studies</li>
                <li>**Setting:** Administered with psychological support/therapy ("set and setting"); not simple pill therapy</li>
                <li>**Timeline:** Likely approved 2025–2027 for clinical use; currently research only</li>
            </ul>

            
            ## 7. Non-Pharmacological Treatments: Evidence and Efficacy

            <p>Psychotherapy and behavioral interventions are first-line for mild-to-moderate depression and essential adjuncts to medication in severe depression.</p>

            <div class="ba-therapy-grid">
                <div class="ba-therapy-card">
                    <div class="ba-therapy-icon">🧠</div>
                    <div class="ba-therapy-title">Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)</div>
                    <p>Challenge automatic thoughts and modify behavior; typically 12–16 sessions. Response 50–60%; comparable to SSRI monotherapy.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-therapy-card">
                    <div class="ba-therapy-icon">💬</div>
                    <div class="ba-therapy-title">Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)</div>
                    <p>Address interpersonal conflict, role transitions, grief, and social deficits; typically 16 sessions. Response 60%; especially effective for role-related depression.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-therapy-card">
                    <div class="ba-therapy-icon">🎯</div>
                    <div class="ba-therapy-title">Behavioral Activation</div>
                    <p>Systematic engagement in valued activities; counters avoidance. Simple, brief (8–12 sessions); response 60–70% for mild-moderate depression.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-therapy-card">
                    <div class="ba-therapy-icon">🧘</div>
                    <div class="ba-therapy-title">Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)</div>
                    <p>Combines mindfulness meditation and CBT; originally for recurrence prevention. Response 50–60%; especially for rumination and relapse prevention.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-therapy-card">
                    <div class="ba-therapy-icon">💭</div>
                    <div class="ba-therapy-title">Psychodynamic Therapy</div>
                    <p>Address unconscious conflicts and patterns. Response 50–60%; may take longer (20+ sessions) but durable gains. Effective for complex trauma-related depression.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="ba-therapy-card">
                    <div class="ba-therapy-icon">☀️</div>
                    <div class="ba-therapy-title">Light Therapy</div>
                    <p>10,000 lux exposure × 30 min morning. First-line for seasonal depression; response 70–80%. Also studied in non-seasonal depression and bipolar.</p>
                </div>
            </div>

            ### Behavioral and Lifestyle Interventions

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Exercise</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Efficacy:** 30 min aerobic activity 5× weekly equivalent to SSRI; dose-response relationship</li>
                    <li>**Mechanism:** Increases BDNF, serotonin, endorphins; improves HPA function; sleep benefit</li>
                    <li>**Evidence:** Meta-analyses show moderate-to-large effect sizes; recommended as first-line or adjunct</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Sleep Hygiene and Sleep Deprivation</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Sleep restriction:** Sleep deprivation induces rapid mood improvement in ~60% of depressed patients (paradoxically); effect typically wanes after one night of recovery sleep</li>
                    <li>**Chronotherapy:** Sleep phase advance (earlier bedtime) or circadian rhythm alignment helps especially in seasonal depression</li>
                    <li>**Insomnia treatment:** Cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) as adjunct; untreated sleep apnea worsens depression outcomes</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Social Rhythm Therapy and Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT)</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Mechanism:** Regular sleep-wake schedule, meal times, social activities stabilize mood and circadian function</li>
                    <li>**Evidence:** Effective as maintenance therapy; particularly beneficial in bipolar depression and seasonal patterns</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Nutritional Approaches</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Omega-3 fatty acids:** Mixed evidence; some benefit in moderate depression; >2 g EPA+DHA daily potentially adjunctive</li>
                    <li>**Folic acid and B vitamins:** Deficiency associated with poor treatment response; supplementation reasonable, especially if low baseline levels</li>
                    <li>**Gut-directed interventions:** Probiotics, prebiotics, fiber show preliminary benefit in depression (through microbiota modification); emerging area</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Meditation and Mindfulness</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT):** 50–60% response; effective for rumination and recurrence prevention</li>
                    <li>**Mechanism:** Reduces engagement with depressive thoughts; activates anterior cingulate and prefrontal cortex</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            
            ## 8. Clinical Management Overview and Key Takeaways

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Initial Assessment Checklist</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>Structured diagnostic interview (PHQ-9, HAM-D); confirm ≥5 symptoms × ≥2 weeks</li>
                    <li>Assess severity: suicidality, psychosis, catatonia, functional impairment</li>
                    <li>Rule out bipolar spectrum (MDQ, personal/family hx of mania)</li>
                    <li>Screen for secondary causes: TSH, B12, folate, CBC, substance use, medications, sleep apnea (STOP-BANG)</li>
                    <li>Assess past response and tolerability to antidepressants</li>
                    <li>Evaluate comorbidities (anxiety, PTSD, substance use, medical conditions)</li>
                    <li>Document baseline weight, metabolic parameters, EKG if >60 yo or cardiac risk</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Treatment Selection by Severity</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Mild:** Psychotherapy, exercise, or SSRI monotherapy; watchful waiting acceptable with strong social support</li>
                    <li>**Moderate:** SSRI/SNRI + psychotherapy (combined most effective); venlafaxine for melancholic features</li>
                    <li>**Severe with psychosis:** SSRI + atypical antipsychotic; ECT urgently if suicidal or catatonic</li>
                    <li>**Severe with suicidality:** Hospitalization if imminent risk; ketamine, ECT, or intensive outpatient program (IOP)</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Monitoring During Antidepressant Trial</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Week 1–2:** Check for adverse effects, activation, behavioral changes, suicidality (especially youth)</li>
                    <li>**Week 4:** First efficacy assessment; expect modest improvement (PHQ-9 decrease of 2–3 points)</li>
                    <li>**Week 8–12:** Major reassessment; if ≥50% response, continue to 12–16 weeks for full remission attempt</li>
                    <li>**Partial response:** Consider dose increase, switch, or augmentation strategy</li>
                    <li>**No response:** Verify adherence, adequate dosing/duration, lack of substance use; change strategy by week 12</li>
                </ul>
            </div>

            <div class="ba-summary-box">
                <h4>Maintenance and Relapse Prevention</h4>
                <ul>
                    <li>**Duration:** Continue antidepressant ≥6–12 months after remission for first episode; consider indefinite for recurrent depression (≥3 episodes or 2 if severe)</li>
                    <li>**Psychotherapy:** Maintenance CBT, IPT, or MBCT reduces relapse ~20–30%</li>
                    <li>**Lifestyle:** Exercise, regular sleep-wake schedule, social connection, stress management</li>
                    <li>**Discontinuation:** Gradual taper (10% reduction per 1–2 weeks) to avoid discontinuation syndrome</li>
                </ul>
            </div>
